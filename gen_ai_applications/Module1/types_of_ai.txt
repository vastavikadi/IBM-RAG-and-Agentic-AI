There are two types of AI: Discriminative AI and Generative AI.

- Discriminative AI focuses on distinguishing between different classes or categories of data. It learns to predict the probability of a label given the input features. Examples of discriminative models include logistic regression, support vector machines, and neural networks used for classification tasks. Email spam filters and image recognition systems are common applications of discriminative AI, where the model is trained to classify inputs into predefined categories. Cannot understand context and cannot generate new contents.

- Generative AI, on the other hand, focuses on generating new data samples that resemble the training data. It learns the underlying distribution of the data and can create new instances that are similar to the original dataset. Examples of generative models include generative adversarial networks (GANs), variational autoencoders (VAEs), transformers, diffusion models, and certain types of recurrent neural networks (RNNs) used for sequence generation tasks. Generative AI is used in applications such as image synthesis, text generation, and music composition, where the model can produce new content that mimics the style and characteristics of the training data.

- Both are created using Deep and Neural Networks, but they serve different purposes. Discriminative AI is primarily used for classification and prediction tasks, while Generative AI is used for creating new data and content.

- LLMs (Large Language Models) are a subset of Generative AI that are specifically designed to understand and generate human-like text. They are trained on vast amounts of text data and can perform a variety of language-related tasks, such as translation, summarization, question answering, and conversation. LLMs leverage advanced architectures like transformers to capture complex patterns in language, enabling them to produce coherent and contextually relevant responses.
